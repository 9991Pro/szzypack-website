Getting your artwork right before it reaches the printer saves time, money, and frustration. Flexible packaging printing has specific technical requirements that differ from digital design — understanding them upfront prevents costly rework and production delays.

## The Golden Rules of Packaging Artwork

Before opening Illustrator, know these non-negotiable rules:

1. **CMYK, not RGB.** Screens use RGB; printers use CMYK. Colors shift when converted — sometimes dramatically. Design in CMYK from the start.
2. **300 DPI minimum.** All raster images must be at least 300 DPI at actual print size. A 72 DPI web image will look pixelated and unprofessional when printed.
3. **5 mm bleed on all sides.** The bleed is the area beyond the trim line that gets cut off. Without it, slight cutting misalignment leaves white edges on your bag.
4. **Embed or outline all fonts.** If the printer does not have your font, the file will substitute — and the result is unpredictable. Convert text to outlines before sending.

## File Format Requirements

| Requirement | Specification |
|------------|---------------|
| **File format** | Adobe Illustrator (.ai) with PDF compatibility, or press-ready PDF/X-4 |
| **Color mode** | CMYK (not RGB, not LAB) |
| **Resolution** | 300 DPI minimum for all raster elements |
| **Bleed** | 5 mm beyond trim on all edges |
| **Safety margin** | 5 mm inside trim for all critical text and logos |
| **Fonts** | All text converted to outlines or fonts embedded |
| **Linked images** | Embedded (not linked externally) |
| **Pantone/Spot colors** | Specify if required; otherwise converted to CMYK |

## Understanding the Dieline

A dieline is the template that shows where your bag will be cut, folded, and sealed. Your supplier provides this as an .ai or .pdf file with clearly marked layers:

- **Cut line** (red) — where the bag is trimmed
- **Fold lines** (green) — where gussets fold inward
- **Seal areas** (blue) — where heat seals go; no critical artwork here
- **Bleed area** — area beyond the cut line that gets trimmed off

**Critical rule:** Keep all text, logos, and important elements at least 5 mm inside the cut line. Anything closer risks being trimmed off or folded into a seal.

## Common Mistakes That Delay Production

### 1. RGB Color Mode

The most common mistake. A vibrant blue in RGB can become a dull purple in CMYK. Always convert and check the CMYK preview before submitting. Our team can provide physical color proofs on the actual film material — request this for your first order to calibrate expectations.

### 2. Missing Bleed

Background colors and images must extend 5 mm past the cut line. If the background stops at the cut line, any slight misalignment in cutting produces white slivers on the edge of your bag.

### 3. Text Too Close to Cut Line

If your text is 1-2 mm from the cut line, it WILL get cut into on some bags. This is not a printer error — it is normal manufacturing tolerance (±1-2 mm). Keep text 5 mm inside.

### 4. Using Thin Fonts for Small Text

Fonts with thin strokes disappear at small sizes on flexible film. Minimum recommended: 6 pt for sans-serif, 8 pt for serif fonts. Avoid reverse text (white text on dark background) below 8 pt.

### 5. Black Not Set to Rich Black

100% K (black) alone prints as dark grey, not true black. Use a rich black: C=40 M=30 Y=30 K=100 for large black areas. For small body text, use 100% K to avoid registration issues.
